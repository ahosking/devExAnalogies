## Health and Family Analogies

A collection of health and personal analogies for explaining DevEx concepts to skeptical audiences. Think of it as the "but actually, no" guide to productivity metrics, because if measuring family love taught us anything, it's that the most important things are often the hardest to quantify, and that's okay.

### Blood test: healthy ranges

Medical tests show when you're outside a healthy range, not targets to optimize infinitely.

How it applies:

- A blood test doesn't say "minimize cholesterol to zero." It shows normal ranges.
- Metrics should identify when something is wrong, not be optimized endlessly.
- Cycle time has a healthy range: too fast means corner-cutting, too slow means blocked.

> "Like a blood test, we're looking for a healthy range. We don't want cycle time at zero, we want it in the healthy range for our organization. Too fast, and we're cutting corners. Too slow, and we're blocked."

Works well with: executives and managers. Reframes metrics as diagnostic tools, not scorecards. Prevents the "make the number go down forever" mentality.

---

### Family love: why qualitative data matters

Use intangibles to show why qualitative data matters. Developer experience is "intangible" like love. You can't measure it with pure telemetry, but it's real and measurable through human judgment.

> "Ask them: how much do you love your family? Give me an objective measure."
> When they can't: "Exactly. But you know if you love your family. That's qualitative data that matters."

When someone says "we can only trust quantitative data":
> "How much do you love your family? Give me an objective measure. You can't, but you know it's real. Developer experience is similar: we measure it through human assessment, not just computers."

Works well with: data skeptics and quantitatively-focused leaders. Creates an "aha moment" about qualitative metrics.

---

### Athletic coaching: individual and system performance

Like sports coaches, DevEx leaders need to develop individual performance alongside system performance.

- Coaches work on team dynamics and individual athlete development.
- System improvements alone aren't enough.
- Individual developer capability, focus, and cognitive capacity matter too.

> "Like a football coach, we need to fix the system (the playbook, the field, the equipment) and develop individual player capability. Both matter."
> "But there's a limit to how long someone can focus. You can't have a developer in deep work for 8 hours straight. They'll be exhausted after 3. Even with AI helping, we need to respect cognitive capacity."

---

### Peak fitness: cognitive limits

Knowledge work productivity is like athletic fitness. There are natural limits to cognitive capacity.

- You can't run at max speed forever. Athletes need recovery.
- Developers have cognitive limits. They can't code intensely all day.
- Removing friction helps, but doesn't remove biological limits.

> "Developers are like athletes. You can't expect 8 hours of continuous intense coding. After 3 hours of deep work, cognitive capacity drops. Removing interruptions and friction helps, but we can't ignore biological limits."

Explains why "just work harder" isn't the answer. Legitimizes breaks, deep work blocks, and context switching costs.

---

### Health checkup: the portfolio approach

A comprehensive health checkup doesn't rely on one number. Blood pressure, cholesterol, glucose, and heart rate all matter. Singular focus on one is like obsessing over weight alone.

- Metrics need multiple dimensions: speed, quality, impact, experience.
- Cherry-picking one metric is like cherry-picking one vital sign.
- A portfolio approach is much harder to game.

> "A doctor doesn't judge your health on one number. They look at blood pressure, cholesterol, heart rate, glucose. Same with productivity: we need multiple indicators, not just cycle time."

---

### Health insurance: incentive structures

Bad incentive structures are like broken health insurance. They reward the wrong behaviors.

- If you incentivize surgeries per doctor, they'll recommend unnecessary surgeries.
- If you incentivize PRs per developer, they'll create trivial PRs.
- Incentive structure determines behavior, not good intentions.

Explains how metrics gaming happens. It's not malice, it's incentives. Shows why framing matters from day one.

## When to use each

| Analogy | Best for | Strength | Limitation |
| ------- | -------- | -------- | ---------- |
| Blood Test | Executives, managers | Intuitive, prevents infinite optimization | Can oversimplify |
| Family Love | Data skeptics, quant-focused leaders | Creates "aha moment" on qualitative data | Can feel sentimental |
| Athletic Coaching | Technical teams, engineers | Resonates with high performers | Needs sports context |
| Peak Fitness | Anyone discussing cognitive limits | Grounds in biological reality | Less relevant outside knowledge work |
| Health Checkup | Metrics portfolio discussions | Comprehensive framing | Takes longer to explain |
| Health Insurance | Incentive structure discussions | Explains gaming naturally | Can get politicized |

## Key insights

1. Health analogies resonate with executives who understand medicine better than software metrics.
2. The family/love example shifts thinking about "soft" data without being dismissive of it.
3. Peak fitness shows why infinite productivity is impossible, which is useful for setting realistic expectations.
4. Incentive structure determines outcomes, not good intentions.
5. Multiple vital signs beat a single number every time.
